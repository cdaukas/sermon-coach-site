The Moses comparison is your best exegetical work — you resist the obvious "Moses bad, Jesus good" move and read the passage as good vs. better, glory vs. greater glory. The Greek note on apostolos earns its keep. The not-guilty verdict imagery at 12:43 is gospel preaching at its best — but it lands at minute 12 inside the identity section, then doesn't return with the same force. By the closing invitation, the gospel becomes a password ("in Christ alone through faith alone by grace alone") rather than a fresh proclamation.
Two Growth Opportunities
Gospel becomes a moment, not a thread. The verdict imagery does its work and exits. Practical step: mark every place in the manuscript where the gospel is named explicitly. For future sermons, state the gospel in the opening, the middle of the explanation, the application, and the close — same gospel, four different angles. The listener never gets more than 10 minutes from a fresh statement of good news.
The biblical author's therefore isn't fully exploited. Hebrews argues Jesus > Moses because the readers are about to make a category mistake. Practical step: on argumentative texts, write one prep sentence: "The author is doing X because his readers are tempted to Y." Then make sure your sermon does the same X for the same reason.

Highest-leverage place to grow. Three competing FCFs run in the sermon: misplaced loyalty (Bears opening), cost of discipleship (minute 5), and drift through ease (minute 35). The third is closest to what the text is actually diagnosing — but it arrives too late to anchor the sermon. Your two-point outline ("identity / consider Jesus") obscures rather than reveals the text's melodic line, which builds through seven beats: consider Jesus → sent and faithful → just as Moses → but greater glory → because son not servant → and we are the house → if we hold fast.
Two Growth Opportunities
One FCF, one sentence, present tense. Right now there are three. Practical step: before preaching this passage again, write the FCF as one sentence — "We are tempted to trade Christ for a loyalty that costs less, even when the trade is actually a downgrade." Tape it to the top of the manuscript. Test every paragraph against it. The Bears story is great but serves "misplaced loyalty" — it needs one extra sentence to serve the actual FCF.
Structure should follow the text's argument, not topic categories. Practical step: on argumentative texts, outline the sermon as the steps in the text's argument rather than as topical points. The sermon length is the same; listeners feel the build instead of the categorization. Memorability comes from feeling an argument move.

Where the sermon comes alive. The Bears opening is one of the strongest moves — specific, present-tense, costly-loyalty-themed. The cultural diagnosis at 5:12 ("biblical understanding of marriage... going to the lake every weekend... especially in Phoenix") is exactly the present-tense, located, named application this rubric is designed to reward. You're preaching to Phoenix in 2021, not to "the modern Christian." The weaker side: in the closing application, the specific person disappears. You list categories — career, family, trips — but no one is in their kitchen the way you were with the Bears.
Two Growth Opportunities
Show the person, don't list the categories. Practical step: before writing the application section, write one sentence: "The specific person this paragraph is for is ____." Fill the blank with someone you can see — the 38-year-old dad whose Sunday morning is now optional because club soccer went year-round. Write the application to that person in second person. Generalize for delivery, but keep the sketch's specificity.
The escape-room metaphor undercuts your invitation. At 38:31 you've built for 37 minutes toward a direct question — and then you ask "how many people here have done an escape room?" Practical step: track your "weight moments" in the manuscript. Ask of each: am I about to lighten this with a metaphor or aside? If yes, ask why. Sometimes the answer is "the weight is too much and I'm rescuing the listener." The next invitation, let the question sit. 30 seconds of weighted silence does more than a clever metaphor.

The "gathering as church membership instead of going to the lake" line (5:34) is 9Marks-shaped at its best — costly loyalty framed without scolding. The "we are his house" pivot does real ecclesiological work. Expository exultation is mixed — the not-guilty verdict reads as genuine awe, and "Jesus never let God the Father down, never not once" lands with feeling. But across 41 minutes, the affection is intermittent. The 8:55 preface ("I've had this sense that God wants to do something") signals that you want to be affected, which is a tell that the affection isn't quite in the sermon itself yet.
Two Growth Opportunities
The 8:55 preface is doing work the sermon should do. Announcing that God wants to do something in hearts is a request the listener bring affection. The sermon should bring it to them. Practical step: when you want to write a preface like this, ask what specifically you sense God wants to do — then write that directly into the sermon at the right place. The preface is a tell that there's weight available the sermon isn't fully delivering.
Affection peaks early and flattens through the Moses argument. Cross section (22:43-24:31) is your exultation peak; minutes 24-32 are teaching-shaped, not worship-shaped. Practical step: in extended teaching sections, write yourself a "doxological interruption" — one paragraph that pauses the argument to exult in what's just been shown. Around minute 28, after establishing Jesus's greater glory, 90 seconds on what that means for worshipping him right now gives the argument a heart.

Move the gospel from a moment into a thread
The not-guilty verdict imagery at 12:43 is gospel preaching at full strength. Don't let it be the only place. Three more touches of that same vividness — different angles on the same gospel — keep the listener inside the good news during the teaching sections.
Practical StepMark every gospel statement in the manuscript. Aim for four — opening, mid-explanation, application, close — each one a fresh angle, not a repeat of the formula.
